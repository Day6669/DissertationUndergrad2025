Starmer faces Labour revolt over backing for oilfield

Jessica Elgot
Kiran stacey
Helena Horton

Keir Starmer is facing a growing internal backlash over the potential approval of a giant new oilfield after Treasury sources indicated Rachel Reeves was likely to back the idea. MPs described a "breaking point" in relations and called for Starmer to reiterate his own commitments to no further oil and gas licences. The Rosebank development was given the go-ahead in 2023 but ruled unlawful by a court last week.

The energy secretary, Ed Miliband, has previously described the licence for Rosebank as "climate vandalism" - setting up a potential clash between his department and the Treasury. Reeves is understood to be supportive of a new application for environmental consent, with allies suggesting that this would not violate Labour's manifesto, which promised not to issue new exploration licences but not to cancel ones already issued. In spring, Miliband's department is due to publish new guidelines that could scupper any attempt by the oil companies to successfully resubmit their applications.

Climate concerned MPs are likely to appeal directly to the prime minister about the importance of being seen to stick to the manifesto commitment of no new oil and gas licences. Anger is widespread even among centrist Labour MPs who have been champions of infrastructure. It is understood the Labour Growth Group - a large caucus of mostly new Labour MPs who have pushed hard on announcements on housing and infrastructure - will not take a position backing Rosebank. The group did back the Heathrow expansion plan announced by Reeves last week. MPs said more criticism would be forthcoming if approval was being pushed by the Treasury. Lung cancer in people who have never smoked cigarettes or tobacco is now estimated to be the fifth highest cause of cancer deaths worldwide, according to the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC).
