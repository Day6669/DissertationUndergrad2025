NATO: UK must boost cash for defence after Trump warning

By JASON GROVES Political Editor

Published: 22:35, 3 February 2025 Updated: 00:44, 4 February 2025

NATO chief Mark Rutte last night urged Keir Starmer to step up defence spending to help placate Donald Trump – as he warned the military alliance could not survive without American support.

Mr Rutte indicated that Nato accepted President Trump’s analysis that member states need to spend 5 per cent of their total GDP on defence.

Standing alongside Sir Keir at a press conference at the Nato headquarters in Brussels, Mr Rutte said Nato countries had increased defence spending by 18 per cent over the last year. But he warned that the alliance’s current 2 per cent spending target would have to be increased.

‘In a more dangerous world, 2 per cent will not be enough to keep us safe,’ he said. ‘We need to invest considerably more.’

Mr Trump has demanded that Nato countries increase defence spending after years of relying on the US.

Sir Keir acknowledged the need for European Nato members to ‘shoulder more of the burden now’. He said the UK was already spending 2.3 per cent of its GDP on defence and insisted he was ‘working hard to set a path to 2.5 per cent’.

Labour committed to the 2.5 per cent target before the general election but has torn up a Tory timetable to meet it by the end of the decade. A new strategic defence review is currently underway. But tight public finances have led the Treasury to propose delaying the target until 2032. At a dinner with EU leaders last night, Sir Keir said European states needed to improve collaboration in the face of increasing global threats.

The Prime Minister highlighted the risk of Russia targeting vital undersea cables around Europe’s coastline. ‘We need to deepen cooperation to protect ourselves from state threat and sabotage, including on subsea infrastructure,’ Sir Keir said.

‘We need to improve military mobility and logistics across Europe so that we can move troops and material more easily to where they need to be.’ Mr Rutte played down suggestions that Mr Trump’s demand to take Greenland from Denmark could splinter the Nato alliance, saying there were ‘always issues between allies’.
